i360 Calls for iOS and Android

UXvisual design
Click on an image to enlarge.

My employer, i360, needed a mobile front end to its Predictive Dialer application. The Predictive Dialer lets customers —typically political campaigns or advocacy groups — administer phone surveys to targeted lists of voters. The Predictive Dialer would, in its normal configuration, place calls on the server. When calls are connected, the server routes each call to a user, usually a campaign volunteer, using a client app.

In addition to connecting the user to phone calls routed from the server, it allows the user to submit survey answers or other information such as wrong numbers or refusals to participate.

i360 decided that, in addition to revamping its aging Predictive Dialer web client as i360 Calls for Web, we should also develop Android and iOS versions. These would allow volunteers to give phone surveys using their own devices. Customers also wanted deploy our apps onto inexpensive devices such as iPad Minis in order to run onsite “phone” banks.

I was responsible for all phases of design for this app, from wireframes to design comps for iOS and Android through testing for design issues, as well as writing copy for prompts, labels, and error messages.

Once connected to the server, the app’s UI consisted not only of the display but also spoken instructions and audio cues. I designed the app to complement and reinforce the server-based audio portion of the application.

As these apps were being designed, Apple had for some time been encouraging developers to use Auto Layout and, with the introduction of the iPhone 6 and 6+, it was clear that we would need to be ready for an expanding number of arbitrary screen sizes, possibly including currently-unknown future sizes. For this reason I directed the design staff to use Auto Layout rather coding for fixed screen sizes as we had done in our previous apps.

I provided all necessary graphic assets to the development team.

I tried to be faithful to each platform in terms aesthetics and in the use of platform UI conventions, like Android’s Action Bar, while keeping the app’s design consistent across platforms. I designed the Android app to conform to the Holo Light UI theme, which was Google’s official recommendation before the advent of Material Design.

Our iOS and Android developers were highly competent programmers but needed plenty guidance to accurately translate the design mocks to the finished app. I would provide feedback in the form of detailed visual guides as the development work progressed. These also helped our QA team to validate the visual-design aspects of the app.

I used a number of my own devices, including several running iOS and one Android tablet, to help test a variety of screen sizes and models.

At the time I left the company, the app was in its final testing and bug-fixing phase. The app has by now entered production and has been used by thousands of users.
