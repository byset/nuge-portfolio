Zhou Family Was Influential in Jiangsu City before Corruption Inquiry

Zhou Bin's uncle prospered in Wuxi until late last year, when investigators raided a home, taking away a number of safes and gold bars

By staff reporter Xie Haitao


Zhou Yuanqing's home in Wuxi, in the eastern province of Jiangsu

(Wuxi) – On February 12, the funeral for Zhou Yuanxing was held at his home in the Xiqiantou Village, in Wuxi, in the eastern province of Jiangsu. More than a hundred people attended the funeral, but not his brothers.

Zhou Yuanxing, who died aged 69 of cancer, was the second of three brothers. Media reports on March 3 say his younger brother, Zhou Yuanqing, and brother's wife, Zhou Lingying, were taken away in early December by investigators from Beijing.

His nephew, Zhou Bin, is the son of his older brother and is also embroiled in a corruption inquiry. Zhou Bin is the former chairman of Beijing Zhongxu Yangguang Petroleum and Natural Gas Technology Ltd. He is involved in the country's hydropower and oil businesses.

The Zhou family has been influential in Wuxi. The eldest brother, whose original name was Zhou Yuangen, left home at an early age to go to Beijing for school and work. He later changed his name to Zhou Yongkang and became a member of the Politburo Standing Committee, the Communist Party's top decision-making body. He retired in 2012.

The youngest brother was deputy director of a district land and resources bureau in Wuxi. His wife is a well-known businesswoman engaged in natural gas and auto dealership business.

Caixin learned that most of the Zhou brothers' family members have been put in custody.

Zhou Yuanxing was diagnosed of bone cancer in the fall. In December, his house was raided twice and assets were confiscated. Villagers say officials who handled the inspections said Zhou faced a charge of "holding a huge amount assets with unknown origin."

Witnesses said many safes, boxes of expensive liquor and gold bars were confiscated.

The Zhou Brothers

In the early 1960s, when the oldest Zhou left home to study at the former Beijing Oil College, Zhou Yuanxing and Zhou Yuanqing graduated from middle school and stayed home to farm.

Zhou Yuanqing later moved to Wuxi and, with the help of his father-in-law, got a job in the local government.

Zhou Yuanxing stayed in the village but his lot had soon improved with help from his brothers.

"Zhou Yuanxing's family boomed so fast," one villager said. "When his older brother worked in China National Petroleum Corp. (CNPC), he became rich. He smoked expensive cigarettes and drank pricy liquor."

Villagers said that Zhou Yuanxing was running a dealership selling Wuliangye, a brand of liquor from the southwestern province of Sichuan.

People went to Zhou Yuanxing and his family with gifts and money for all kinds of requests. "For instance, if someone wanted to get a job or a company needed to solve some trouble, people would go to them for help," another villager said.

A county official in Wuxi once paid 150,000 yuan to the Zhous for help in a lawsuit, a source close to the family said. A recommendation from the Zhous has also helped students who failed an exam to enter a police school in Jiangsu, sources said.

Auto Dealership

Villagers say Zhou Yuanqing's wife, Zhou Lingying, is involved in mining, buying oil field equipment and operating gas stations.

Business document shows that Zhou Lingying is the controller of Beijing Honghan Co., a major shareholder in Beijing-based Hongfeng Investment Co., which in 2007 partnered with a Sichuan subsidiary of CNPC to set up Hongfeng Potash Co. with 300 million yuan.

In 2010, Hongfeng Potash received mining rights from Sichuan land and resources officials to develop potash, boron, halite and other minerals in an area of 13.8 square kilometers until November 2015.

Company documents show that as of June 2011, the valuation of Hongfeng Potash's mining rights was 715 million yuan.

In 2010, Zhou Lingying invested 19 million yuan to set up Jiangyin Benyue Auto Co. as an auto part supplier. The company later became the city's only Audi dealer.

"In the auto industry, everyone knows that the Audi dealership is very profitable," an auto dealer in Beijing said. "It not only requires money, but also strong connections."

Jiangyin Benyue is also a partner in a CNPC subsidiary called Kunlun Energy, which is involved in the liquefied gas business. Starting last year, a number of CNPC executives have stepped down amid an anti-corruption storm, including Li Hualin, deputy general manager of CNPC and chairman of Kunlun Energy.
